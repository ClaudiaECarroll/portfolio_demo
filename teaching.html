Teaching

Fostering critical thinking about technology, culture, and digital methods in humanities education

Teaching Philosophy

My approach to teaching digital humanities emphasizes critical engagement with technology rather than mere technical skill acquisition. I believe students need to understand not just how to use digital tools, but how to think critically about their social, cultural, and political implications.

I design courses that balance hands-on technical work with theoretical reflection, encouraging students to see digital methods as interpretive practices rather than neutral techniques. My pedagogy emphasizes collaborative learning, iterative experimentation, and reflective practice.

Central to my teaching philosophy is the commitment to inclusive pedagogy that recognizes different learning styles, technical backgrounds, and cultural perspectives. I work to create classroom environments where students feel empowered to take intellectual risks while developing both technical competencies and critical analytical skills.

Current Courses

Undergraduate • Fall 2024

Introduction to Digital Humanities

Course Number: DH 201 • Enrollment: 25 students

This foundational course introduces students to key concepts, methods, and debates in digital humanities. Students learn basic computational skills (Python, web scraping, data visualization) while engaging with critical questions about the role of technology in humanities scholarship.

Key Learning Objectives:

  • Understand the historical development and current scope of digital humanities
  • Develop basic programming and data analysis skills
  • Critically analyze digital humanities projects and methodologies
  • Create an original digital humanities project proposal

Major Assignments: Weekly lab exercises, critical project reviews, collaborative data analysis project, final project proposal with prototype

Python Programming Data Visualization Critical Theory Project-Based Learning
Graduate Seminar • Fall 2024

Critical Algorithm Studies

Course Number: DH 650 • Enrollment: 12 students

This graduate seminar examines algorithms as cultural and political artifacts. Students engage with scholarship from science and technology studies, critical data studies, and platform studies to understand how algorithmic systems shape social relations and cultural practices.

The course combines theoretical readings with hands-on analysis of algorithmic systems. Students learn to "read" algorithms critically by examining their social contexts, embedded values, and cultural effects.

Key Topics: Algorithmic bias and fairness, platform capitalism, surveillance systems, recommendation algorithms, automated content moderation, algorithmic governance

Final Project: Original research paper analyzing a specific algorithmic system using methods from critical algorithm studies

Critical Theory Platform Studies Research Methods Algorithm Analysis

Regularly Offered Courses

Undergraduate • Spring Semester

Digital Culture and Society

Course Number: CULT 350

An interdisciplinary examination of how digital technologies are reshaping contemporary culture. Topics include social media and identity, digital labor, platform capitalism, surveillance culture, and resistance practices in digital spaces.

Students engage with case studies ranging from influencer culture to digital activism, developing skills in cultural analysis and media criticism. No prior technical background required.

Assessment Methods: Discussion leadership, cultural analysis essays, multimedia final project

Cultural Studies Media Analysis Social Media Digital Culture
Graduate Seminar • Spring Semester

Computational Text Analysis for Humanists

Course Number: DH 635

This methods-focused seminar teaches graduate students how to apply computational approaches to textual analysis while maintaining humanistic interpretive practices. Students learn techniques including topic modeling, sentiment analysis, network analysis, and machine learning applications for text.

Emphasis on critical engagement with computational methods, understanding their assumptions and limitations, and developing hybrid methodologies that combine computational and traditional interpretive approaches.

Technical Skills: Python, R, natural language processing libraries, statistical analysis, data visualization

Text Mining Python Natural Language Processing Statistical Analysis
Undergraduate/Graduate • Alternate Years

Digital Archives and Cultural Memory

Course Number: DH 420/620

Students explore how digital technologies are transforming the preservation, organization, and accessibility of cultural materials. The course examines both technical and theoretical aspects of digital archiving, including metadata design, digitization workflows, and the politics of digital preservation.

Students work with local cultural institutions to gain hands-on experience with digital archiving projects while engaging with critical scholarship on archives, memory, and power.
